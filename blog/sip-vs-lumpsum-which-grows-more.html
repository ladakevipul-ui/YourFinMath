SIP vs Lumpsum — Which Grows More in 10 Years?

Real growth numbers, a built-in calculator, and an honest answer for Indian investors — so you stop guessing and start investing smarter.

📅 May 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 📊 Includes live calculator

You have money to invest. The question is — should you put it all in at once (lumpsum) or spread it out every month (SIP)? This is one of the most common questions from Indian mutual fund investors, and the answer is not as simple as most people think.

This article gives you the real numbers, the honest pros and cons, and a calculator to compare both strategies for your specific situation.

Quick answer: If you invest the same total amount, lumpsum generally grows more over the long term — because money starts compounding from day one. But SIP protects you from market timing risk and is far better for most salaried investors who don't have a large sum ready to invest.

What is SIP and What is Lumpsum?

Before comparing, let's be clear on what each means:

📅 SIP (Systematic Investment Plan)

  • You invest a fixed amount every month
  • Example: ₹5,000 every month for 10 years
  • Total invested: ₹6,00,000 over 10 years
  • Works like an EMI but for wealth building
  • Ideal for salaried individuals

💰 Lumpsum

  • You invest the entire amount at once
  • Example: ₹6,00,000 invested on Day 1
  • Total invested: ₹6,00,000 on one day
  • Works best when you have a windfall (bonus, sale, gift)
  • Requires good market timing

What is Rupee Cost Averaging — SIP's Secret Weapon

The biggest advantage of SIP is something called Rupee Cost Averaging (RCA). Here's how it works in simple terms:

Example: You do ₹5,000 SIP every month in a mutual fund.

— In January, NAV is ₹100 → you buy 50 units
— In February, market falls, NAV is ₹80 → you buy 62.5 units (more for same ₹5,000)
— In March, NAV recovers to ₹110 → you buy 45.5 units

Your average purchase cost is lower than if you had bought everything at ₹110. This is rupee cost averaging.

When markets fall, SIP investors don't panic — they actually benefit by buying more units at a lower price. This is why SIP is emotionally and mathematically better for most people during volatile markets.

The Honest Verdict

So which one should YOU choose?

📅
Choose SIP if you're salaried, investing monthly savings, nervous about markets, or a first-time investor. It's the best default for 90% of Indians.
💰
Choose Lumpsum if you have a large amount ready (bonus, sale, inheritance), markets have recently fallen 15–20%, and you have a long time horizon of 7+ years.
🔄
Do both — most smart investors run a regular SIP AND invest lumpsum whenever they get extra money. This is the best strategy overall.
⚠️
Never try to time the market. Even experts can't predict market peaks and bottoms consistently. SIP removes this problem completely.

How to Start — 3 Simple Steps

1

Frequently Asked Questions

Is SIP better than lumpsum for mutual funds in India?
For most salaried Indians, yes — SIP is better because it doesn't require a large sum upfront, builds investing discipline, and protects against market timing risk through rupee cost averaging. Lumpsum is better when you have a large amount and markets are at a correction.
Can I do both SIP and lumpsum in the same mutual fund?
Absolutely. This is actually the smartest approach. Keep your regular monthly SIP going, and whenever you receive a bonus, tax refund, or any windfall — invest that as a lumpsum in the same or a different fund. You get discipline from SIP and maximum compounding from lumpsum.
What is the minimum SIP amount in India?
Most mutual funds allow SIP starting from ₹100 to ₹500 per month. Popular platforms like Groww, Zerodha Coin, and Paytm Money allow SIPs from ₹100. There is no minimum for lumpsum either — typically ₹1,000 to ₹5,000 depending on the fund.
What return rate should I assume for SIP calculations?
For large-cap equity mutual funds, 10–12% per year is a reasonable long-term assumption based on historical Nifty 50 returns. For mid/small cap funds, 12–15% is often used, but these carry more risk. Always use a conservative number (10–12%) for planning.
Is lumpsum investment risky?
Yes, lumpsum carries more short-term risk than SIP — especially if you invest at a market peak. If markets fall 20% right after your lumpsum, your entire invested amount drops. With SIP, only the first installment is affected. For this reason, lumpsum is recommended only with a 7+ year horizon and when markets are not at all-time highs.

The Bottom Line

There's no universally "better" option — it depends on your situation. But here's the simplest rule to follow:

  • Monthly salary → SIP. Every month, auto-invest a fixed amount. Don't think about it.
  • Bonus, windfall, or large savings → Lumpsum. Put it to work immediately and let compounding do the rest.
  • Uncertain about markets → SIP always wins. You'll never regret starting a SIP.

The best investment is the one you actually make. Stop waiting for the "perfect time" — start today with whatever amount you can.
